Dissertation: Using Large Language Models to Match Request for Quotations to Suppliers in a Business-to-Business Supply Chain Procurement Platform

Despite the significant technological advancements which have been introduced in the consumer sales sector, the Business-to-Business (B2B) sector has lagged behind in adopting new technologies. This has resulted in the majority of these processes being slow and reliant on human expertise. This paper examines the implementation of Large Language Models (LLMs) to enhance the efficiency and quality of matching Request for Quotations with suitable suppliers in a B2B supply chain procurement platform. By leveraging LLM vector embeddings and similarity metrics such as cosine similarity, we significantly improve the efficiency of the entity matching processes taking place in these procurement platforms. Through comprehensive experimentation, with the use of various models and approaches, we demonstrate the effectiveness of LLMs within the domain. We develop a system capable of matching RFQs to appropriate suppliers within seconds from a pool of hundreds of companies. With the top-performing model, on average ranking the correct supplier as the second most probable and achieving a success rate of 47.9% in identifying the supplier selected by a procurement agent, we clearly demonstrate the real-world suitability of LLMs to this task. Furthermore, we provide a comparison of the use of open versus closed source LLMs and assess the advantages and disadvantages that such models present. Overall, this paper provides an in-depth analysis of the successful integration of LLMs into the procurement sector, providing insights into improving the efficiency and quality of B2B supply chain procurement platforms.

COM3023 Coursework

This project was completed as part of the COM3023 module at the University of Exeter. This paper aims to investigate the implementation of Bayesian Neural Networks, in particular their use in being used to assess the uncertainty within Neural Network model parameters. It makes use of approximation methods such as Markov Chain Monte Carlo and Variational Inference in order to implement this Bayesian approach. Through the use of the Breast Cancer Wisconsin dataset, we see how such methods are implemented and what benefits they may hold. We also compare the two methods in order to provide an overview of the key differences and capabilities they hold.

ECM3412 ACO

This project was completed as part of the ECM3412 module at the University of Exeter. The project involved implementing an Ant Colony Optimisation algorithm to solve the Travelling Salesman Problem. This project includes Python code containing functions and algorithms for ACO-based TSP solving. It provides functionalities for reading in the data, generating ant paths, updating pheromones, and running the ACO algorithm with different configurations including standard ACO, MMAS and Elitist.
